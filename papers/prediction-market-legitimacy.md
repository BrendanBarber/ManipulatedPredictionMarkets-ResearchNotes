## "Prediction Laundering: The Illusion of Neutrality, Transparency, and Governance in Polymarket" (2026)
[Link](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.05181v1)
Authors: Yasaman Rohanifar, Syed Ishtiaque Ahmed, Sharifa Sultana

**Initial Thoughts:** *Abstract includes the word 'sociotechnical' so this is already going well.*

**Prediction Laundering:** *The sociotechnical process by which individual uncertain bets and strategic hedges are scrubbed of their original noise and personal stakes through market aggregation.*

**RQ1:** *How do prediction market design and pricing mechanisms transform the users’ contested beliefs and value-laden judgments into seemingly neutral probabilistic signals?*

**Initial Q1 Thoughts:** *Wisdom of crowds and the higher confidence of experts/insiders.*

**RQ2:** *How do users interpret, rely on, and contest these probabilistic signals, particularly when moral discomfort, uncertainty, or exclusion arise?*

**Initial Q2 Thoughts:** *I am interested to see if these markets are consistentally viewed as accurate fortune-tellers, or if people disagree based on factors such as political beliefs. I also wonder if the fact that people push back against this markets is what allows them to have accurate prediction, if people saw a 70-30 market and agreed with it, it wouldn't stay 70-30, it would move to 80-20, then 90-10, etc.*

**RQ3:** *What kind of risks and challenges occur for users when reality, rumors, and probabilities as numbers convolute together?*

**Initial Q3 Thoughts:** *I assume there may come an overeliance on this markets for preicting elections, and this overliance will grow until a series of high-profile cases of it being a bad predictor, either due to a natural collapse, or purposeful misinformation/manipulation.*

**4-stage laundering cycle:** 
- Structural Santization: *The platform gets to decide what is bettable.* Although, I would note that they are heavily inclined to create markets that people care about, since that encourages more people to use the platform and also creating markets that don't get bet on is "money losing"
- Probablistic Flattening: *The interface collapes strategic hedging and interpretive oscillation into a single numerical truth.* Basically you can't know why someone made a certain decision. This seems to go against the initial intention of prediction markets, where experts put money behind their actual prediction of the future, and non-experts would filter out through bad decisions. Strategic hedging goes against this, and the shift towards a gambling website encourages non-experts to keep betting long after they have been doing poorly.
- Architectural Masking: *Selective opacity hiding the concentration of capital and whale influences. Creating the mirage of democratic census.* The focus on "wisedom of crowds" ignores that there are a small number of whales who can change the direction of the market and it is up for debate whether these whales are actually experts.
- Epistemic Hardening: *Governance disputes and moral discomfort is erased to produce authoritative facts.* The example mentioned about the "weird discomfort with betting on people dying in a war" that is erased by the way the information is presented as any other market chart.
